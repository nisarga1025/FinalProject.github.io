Thalamus
Unit 8
What It Does
The thalamus is the primary pathway by which sensory information enters the brain. In regard to learning, it is a critical step in fear response pathways, as it transmits bodily sensations to the amygdala from their respective sensory areas. According to Rodriguez's team, the thalamus can do this in two speeds. The faster speed directly conveys sensory information from the thalamus to the amygdala, and the slower speed pathway sends sensory information from the thalamus to the sensory cortex before going to the amygdala. This results in the information being sent to the amygdala having varying quality. The fast pathway sends more crude information to the amygdala, and the slower pathway sends more refined information. This sometimes results in an initial misjudgment of perception, that is then corrected when information from the slower pathway better identifies what is being observed.

Orbitofrontal Cortex
Unit 5
What It Does
The orbitofrontal cortex’s (OFC) main role in learning and memory lies in operant conditioning. In conjunction with the dorsal striatum, which pairs a discriminative stimulus and a response, the OFC links that response to a predicted outcome. Therefore, the dorsal striatum and the OFC form the foundation for operant conditioning in the brain: The dorsal striatum links the stimulus to a response, and that response is then predicted by the OFC, which in turn helps develop an idea of what we think is going to happen.

The OFC is also important in reward pathways in the brain, specifically in deciding which reward to go with. According to the Unit 5 Lesson 4 slide deck, which draws on an experiment outlined by Gluck et al., rats with lesions to the OFC chose to take smaller rewards that came without delay, suggesting that the OFC is useful in deciding which reward system is better to take. This suggests that the OFC is linked to reward pathways, for which the circuitry is outlined a few slides later. The OFC is shown to interact with the Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA) and the Substantia Niagra Pars Compacta (SNC), which are important in dopamine signaling in the brain. This ultimately suggests that the OFC is critical in moderating dopamine production and signaling in the brain and is a critical part of the brain’s reward pathway.​

Locus Coeruleus
Unit 8
What It Does
The locus coerulus is a region of the brainstem, specifically the pons, which releases norephinephrine, a hormone which drives our bodies "fight or flight response". Through the release of NE, activity of the locus coerulus can aid in learning by supporting formation of new memories and strengthening our existing memories. Specifically, the locus coerulus helps us prioritize the encoding of relevant inputs and disregard irrelevant ones.

Evidence of the role of the locus coerulus in memory formation and strengthening of encoding processes can be found in a 2018 study, "Locus Coeruleus Activity Strengthens Prioritized Memories Under Arousal", in which participants were asked to perform a "monetary incentive encoding task" inside of an MRI scanner. In half the trials, a "threat of punishment" was used to induce arousal. Findings showed that in the trials with the "threat of punishment", memory was successfully increased for goal-relevant scenes but not for irrelevant other details.

Basolateral Amygdala
Unit 8
What It Does
The basolateral amygdala is a complex consisting of three of the twelve amygdalar nuclei (lateral, basal, accessory basal) which is an incredibly important component in many neural circuits relating to learning. Firstly, BLA is a crucial intermediate in how we produce emotion via the direct and indirect pathways via the amygdala. In responding to a sensory stimulus, the thalamus sends direct projections to the BLA (direct pathway) or goes through the cortex prior to reaching the BLA, which leads to downstream motor and physiological responses (ANS activation). The BLA acts as a central hub which allows our brains to differentiate between appetitive and aversive stimuli and coordinate our differential responses to each. When we are presented with an appetitive CS, certain neurons are activated which lead to activation of the nucleus accumbens. When we are presented with an aversive stimulus, certain neurons are activated which lead to activation of the central nucleus of the amygdala (CNA). When both are presented, neurons are activated which lead to activation of the ventral hippocampus.​ ​
Evidence Example
Evidence of the role of the Basolateral Amygdala in fear conditioning can be found in a 1996 study which examined the impact the impact of lesioning the BLA on a rats ability to learn fear associations. Rats were conditioned via the pairing of a tone with an aversive foot shock. Results found that the BLA lesions abolished conditional freezing (used as an index of conditioned fear) to all conditioned stimuli, revealing the role of the BLA in expression of conditioned fear.

Suprachiasmatic Nucleus
New / Expanded Region
What It Does
The suprachiasmatic nucleus is critical in sleep regulation, as it is largely responsible for maintaining the body’s circadian rhythm (Ma, 2023). As such, disruptions to the suprachiasmatic nucleus can lead to circadian rhythm disruptions, ultimately resulting in sleep issues for individuals. According to the in-class slide deck, sleep is critical in many processes involving memories. Memories are ”replayed” during sleep, which is believed to be an important part of memory consolidation. Furthermore, sleep is central to the process by which memories encoded in the hippocampus are then placed into the storage of the brain. The suprachiasmatic nucleus allows for this to happen on a regular schedule via moderating the circadian rhythm, ultimately moderating the body to sleep at regular intervals and making this storage process possible. ​
